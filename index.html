A first-principles analysis

Deepinder Goyal says his wearable measures "the cost of being alive." Does the science hold up?

Temple's new metric, "Entropy," promises a live window into how fast you are aging. Stripped to first principles, it is one part real physics, one part outdated biology, and one part marketing. Here is the line between them.

01 / The stated claim

What he actually said

In a public post in June 2026, Goyal said Temple found a biomarker readable only at the temple, measuring "the real-time cost of being alive." He named it Entropy. The specifics:

live number, updates every second scale of 1 to 250 1 = deepest rest (skilled meditators only) 250 = elite athletes at peak output tracked a metabolic cart at r = 0.93 vs heart rate's 0.55 "bring your minimum down every day"

Everything moves it, he says: sleep, stress, a sprint, coffee, a meal, a cold plunge, meditation, strength training. Everything that moves your metabolism. Let us test each piece.

02 / What the metric measures

"Entropy" is a rev counter for your body

In physics, entropy is not a number you can read off a person. It describes a state of disorder, not a live flow. What a sensor can actually track is the rate at which you burn fuel and shed heat. At the whole-body level, that rate has a plain name: your metabolic rate.

So picture a car. The rev counter shows engine RPM right now. Idling quietly at a red light is your body deep in rest. Flooring it on a track is your body at full output. Goyal built a live rev counter for your metabolism and labelled the needle "Entropy." The gauge is real. The label is a glow-up.

why it can beat a heart rate strap

Heart rate lies. Coffee or a tense email spikes your pulse without your body burning more fuel. Real energy burn depends on both how fast blood moves and how much oxygen it delivers (the Fick principle). A blood-flow sensor reads more of that picture than pulse alone, so a tighter match to a true metabolic cart is biologically believable.

03 / Claim-by-claim assessment

Five claims, graded

Tap each claim to see the verdict.

holds upYou are a system that survives only by burning energy›

This is sound physics. A living body is an "open dissipative system." It stays beautifully organised inside only by constantly taking in energy (food, oxygen) and shedding disorder back out as heat and waste. Schrodinger described it in 1944, Prigogine won a Nobel formalising it. The rate of that shedding is, at the whole-body level, your metabolic rate. So "the cost of being alive" as a concept is real and elegant.

mostly holds upIt tracks real energy burn better than heart rate›

Plausible in direction. Blood flow plus oxygen extraction captures more of true oxygen consumption than pulse does. Their claim of r = 0.93 versus heart rate's 0.55 fits that logic.

The asterisks are large, though. It is a company number, not peer-reviewed or independently replicated, and "100+ cardio sessions" is the easy case. Steady rhythmic exercise is simple to estimate. A meal, stress and cold (the hard cases on his own list) are not shown. And brain blood flow is tightly self-regulated, so using a head signal to infer whole-body burn across all of daily life is a genuinely hard inference.

overstatedAcross the animal world, slower burn means longer life›

This is the "rate of living" theory from Rubner (1908) and Pearl (1920s): a fixed lifetime energy budget, so faster burners die sooner. It fits Kleiber's law (bigger animals burn slower per kilo and live longer) and the mouse-versus-elephant intuition.

But the rule breaks on the most important animals. Birds and bats burn hot yet live long. Naked mole-rats burn slow but live so long their lifetime energy spend is many times a mouse's, and their death risk barely rises with age at all. As Speakman's review lays out, the simple version does not hold across species. Invoking it as settled science is a stretch.

true but misreadIn humans, higher resting burn predicts earlier death›

This part has real data. The Baltimore Longitudinal Study followed over 1,200 people for up to 40 years and found higher basal metabolic rate independently predicted higher mortality, up to ~53% higher in the top tier, after adjusting for age, BMI, smoking and diabetes.

But the arrow points the other way. A high resting burn is mostly the smoke, not the fire. It reflects inflammation, stress overdrive and hidden disease making the body work harder. Smashing the thermometer does not cure the fever. The one case where lowering burn looks causal and good is sustained calorie restriction. Low-and-healthy is good. Low-from-illness is not. One number cannot tell them apart.

unproven and riskySo drive your minimum down every single day›

This is the weakest leap. A low resting number is not automatically good: an underactive thyroid, overtraining, starvation and depression all lower it. And a marker is not a lever. Even if resting burn tracked longevity perfectly, chasing the number directly is like smashing a thermometer to cure a fever.

The healthier target is not the lowest floor. It is a low floor plus a high ceiling, covered next.

06 / Metabolic range and VO2 max

It is the range, not the minimum

Goyal sells the floor: get your idle as low as possible. But the longevity-protective profile is a low floor plus a high ceiling. An efficient, calm resting state, and the ability to climb hard when you need to.

The healthy signal is the whole span

a low idle, plus a high peak you can reach
low resting floor = good recovery high ceiling = strong VO2 max
the number that actually predicts your lifespan

That ceiling has a name: VO2 max, your peak aerobic capacity. In a study of 122,000 adults it was inversely tied to death risk with no upper limit of benefit, rivalling or beating classic risk factors. Aging is the ceiling falling, not the floor rising. A device that trains you to obsess over the floor is optimising the less important half.

07 / Assessment summary

The honest scorecard

what is solid

  • The body genuinely survives by dissipating energy. "Cost of being alive" = metabolic rate, and that is a real, meaningful signal.
  • A blood-flow sensor can plausibly estimate that burn better than a heart rate strap.

what is overstated

  • Calling metabolic rate "Entropy" borrows gravitas the measurement has not earned.
  • The cross-species "slow burn, long life" rule is a discredited 1908 theory.
  • Human data is correlational, and cause most likely runs from hidden disease to high burn, not the reverse.

what is unproven

  • The r = 0.93 accuracy outside steady exercise.
  • Head blood flow as a whole-body burn proxy across real life.
  • The whole premise that lowering your resting number is something to chase.
one line to hold it by

Goyal likely built a better, continuous metabolism gauge, then wrapped it in a story that promises far more than a metabolism gauge can deliver. A great sensor and a true story are two different products.
